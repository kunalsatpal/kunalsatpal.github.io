After around 8 months of working at cultfit. I moved to a new team: Team Wellness. And the first and very interesting project that came to us was cross-selling wellness products to fitness users. Of the humongous project, a small part was cart abandonment, an experiment that turned out too good to be true. But firstly


What is cart abandonment?

“Abandonment is an e-commerce term used to describe a visitor on a web page who leaves that page before completing the desired action.”

Shopping cart abandonment refers to visitors who add items to their online shopping cart, but exit without completing the purchase. This somehow feels counterintuitive, If a user has done all the effort of finding, selecting an item and adding it to the cart why won’t he purchase it?

A big and renowned problem in e-commerce

The average cart abandonment rate lies somewhere around 72% which means our of 100 users who add something to the cart only 28 will make payment. This looks like a huge number and probably that’s why e-commerce sites focus so much on a clear visible cart, cart abandonment mailers, push notification, etc

Hypothesis

Our target audience

On cultfit store around 99% the purchases happen within 24hrs of adding something to the cart. We call it a d0 purchase. Post that the purchases are nearly zero for d1 and exactly 0for d2, d3 and so forth.

To improve the overall orders we should help users who have not purchased even after 24 hours window get back to the funnel (have a very low probability of doing it based on data) and help us increease the orders.

Quick testing of hypothesis


And what did we learn

Cart abandonment widget performed really well with around 40X conversions from posters it has replaced. This is due to the fact that these are highly targeted and are shown to the users who have a high probablity of converting.


After a good proof of concept, we decided to make this even more effective by incorporating all the feedback we have received along with the experiment.
